    Lending his considerable star power to get out the vote efforts in Michigan, President Barack Obama Saturday night gave a rousing speech to Democratic supporters, urging them to shake off cynicism and turn around a recent history of staying away from the polls in large numbers in off-year elections.

    Speaking to a crowd of more than 6,000 people at the Matthaei Physical Education Center at Wayne State University, Obama, battling low approval numbers, delivered a passionate address, reminding voters it was a Democratic agenda that helped save the auto industry and increase access to health care.

    "Complaining and not voting, that just means you're giving away your power. You're giving away your precious right to help determine the course of your nation," Obama said, referencing statistics that as many as 900,000 voters in Michigan who vote in presidential years do not in mid-term elections.

    "I don't know what's going on with these folks, but we've got to let those folks know their vote matters," said Obama, who strode onto the stage at Wayne State confidently, his sleeves rolled up and without a tie, hugging Democratic gubernatorial nominee Mark Schauer and U.S. Senate nominee Gary Peters.

    Peters has a considerable edge in the polls to win the seat currently held by U.S. Sen. Carl Levin. Schauer and incumbent Republican governor, Rick Snyder, are locked in a race that may be too close to call, according to the most recent surveys.

    Obama's presence in Michigan comes as one of only a handful of campaign appearances he is making, Peters being the only Democratic nominee for a U.S. Senate seat welcoming the president in the final week of the campaign season. But it didn't seem to slow Obama, whose ability to capture a crowd with his soaring rhetoric is well known.

    Beginning his speech with a rousing "It's good to be back in Detroit" – which brought the house down – Obama launched into an address that both touched on Democratic agenda items including raising the minimum wage, protecting health care reform and cutting taxes, and praised Peters and Schauer as the sort of principled Democrats who can help him finish what he has set out to do.

    "This country has made real progress since the worst economic crisis of our lifetimes," Obama said. "Over the past 4 ½ years we have seen American businesses create more than 10 million new jobs. … I don't have to tell you the auto industry that was on the brink of collapse is back on its feet, making better cars than ever right here in Michigan."

    Michigan Republicans earlier Saturday chided Peters and Schauer for the president's visit, putting out a statement earlier in the day accusing them of "Disregarding the opinion of over 50% of Michiganders who disapprove of the job the president is doing."

    "Gary Peters and Mark Schauer are once again showing their true partisan colors by standing next to President Obama," said Michigan Republican Party Chairman Bobby Schostak.

    There were a few dozen protestors across from the event center, many of them holding anti-abortion signs. But there were no disturbances reported. Supporters of the conservative group Americans for Prosperity also waved signs in opposition to Obama administration policies.

    It was Obama's first visit to Michigan -- which he won in both 2008 and 2012 -- since a speech on the minimum wage he gave in Ann Arbor. His wife, Michelle Obama, visited Detroit for a Democratic rally on Oct. 10.

    Michigan Democratic Party Chairman Lon Johnson has made a mission of drumming up votes from people who vote in presidential years but stay home in off-year elections, and he said Saturday night that the effort is paying off. He said the party has what is believed to be a 100,000-vote edge on Republicans in absentee balloting.

    Obama's speech was preceded by a host of Democratic officeholders or nominees, including Levin, U.S. Sen. Debbie Stabenow, U.S. Rep. John Dingell of Dearborn, Detroit Mayor Mike Duggan and several others.

    "On Tuesday, it doesn't matter who we are and how much money we have in our pockets – a million dollars or one nickel," said Stabenow. "Everybody gets one vote."

    That theme – of outside-the-state spending heavily to influence votes – was one that came up throughout the night, though the speeches failed to note that much more has been spent on Peters' behalf ($20 million) than on Republican U.S. Senate nominee Terri Lynn Land ($15 million). Land's campaign is expected to outspend Peters', however.

    "We're not going to let these folks, these out-of-state billionaires, buy an election in Michigan because the people of the state get to decide who their next U.S. senator is," Peters said, before he introduced the president, and also hit on Democratic themes of raising the minimum wage and protecting collective bargaining rights.

    Schauer, in his speech, reminded supporters that they will decide if he moves Snyder out of the governor's job.

    "Make no mistake, the road to the governor's office runs right through Detroit and it will be decided by you and the people you talk to over the next three days," he said.

    Obama loosened up an enthusiastic crowd, and when he noted that Republicans "are good people, too," someone booed. He responded, "Don't boo, vote."

    "We're all Americans, we're Americans first," he said. "They're patriots, they love this country, but they've got bad ideas."

    His best line of the night may have come when he obliquely referenced Republican opposition to the auto rescue and other actions his administration took in the face of the 2008 recession.

    "Some of the same folks who said six years ago we should have thrown in the towel are now the people asking for your vote," he said. "They got a lot of nerve."
